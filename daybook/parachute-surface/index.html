A surface
for the vault

On making collective memory navigable — what we learned when we asked for a URL and found none, and how we're building the thing that should exist.

Todd Youngblood · RegenHub, LCA — Boulder · July 1, 2026
With Claude Opus 4.7 — drafting, structure, vault integration
In progress — this post is the surface's first artifact

Superficies. Latin: the upper face of a thing — super, above; facies, the face it turns toward you. A surface is not the thing itself. It is what the thing shows.

Today we asked a simple question: where do you go to read the vault's notes? The answer was nowhere. No URL. The vault ships headless — a graph of notes, tags, and links, reachable over MCP and a REST API, with no built-in human interface. This is not a deficiency. It is a design decision, and a good one. The Parachute system's answer to "where do you read notes?" is: you build a surface.

The vault is the interior. The surface is the face it turns toward its members. One is a store of pattern; the other is an act of showing.

But here's what the design decision surfaces (the word will keep doing this): a cooperative that holds its knowledge in a headless vault, navigated only by agents and scripts, has not yet made that knowledge cooperative. Memory without legibility is a private thing, even when it's shared. The surface is not decoration. It's the act of making the interior available to people.

What the vault holds

The co-op's vault currently holds a graph of notes across several registers: governance artifacts (plans, findings, legal notes), daily journals, design decisions, work coordination. These are reachable by path, by tag, or by full-text search — but only if you know to ask, and only through an agent or a script. A member who wants to read the July 1 journal entry has no URL to open.

This is the gap the surface closes. Not by becoming a generic notes app — Notion exists — but by becoming a face that is shaped around how this particular co-op thinks and governs. The surface we build should reflect the LCA's actual structure: its streams of work, its governance rhythm, its documents. Generic surfaces are someone else's judgment about what matters. Ours gets to be ours.

Three views

We're thinking about the organizational surface as three overlapping views, each drawing on different tag families in the vault:

Commons
What the co-op knows
Governance notes, legal artifacts, design decisions, architecture records. The stable layer — things the co-op has settled, made legible, agreed on. Browseable by members, findable without asking an agent.
Stream
What the co-op is thinking
Daily notes, design findings, journal entries — ordered by time. The moving layer. Where observation becomes record. This post will live here.
Work
What the co-op is doing
Sprint notes, open threads, coordination state — linked to Workshop. The coordination layer. Reflects the live state of the collective's attention and commitments.

These aren't rigid sections. They're query families: the surface pulls from the vault's tags, and the vault's tag structure shapes what the surface can show. The two layers design each other.

This post is the first artifact

There is a habit in design of waiting until a thing is finished before writing about it. The surface doesn't exist yet — just a design finding in the vault, a sketch of three views, and this post. We're writing here because the surface should be built in the open, starting from first principles, in the co-op's own journal. The practice is the documentation. The documentation begins the practice.

When the surface exists, it will be findable here. The vault note that prompted this post is at design/parachute-surface-org. The Surface Starter in the vault describes the stack: @openparachute/surface-client for OAuth and vault access, @openparachute/surface-render for note rendering. A React app, built in the editor, deployed somewhere members can reach it.

What comes next

Design the vault's tag structure to support the three views — what tags mark Commons-layer notes, how the Stream is ordered, what links Work notes to Workshop state. Then build auth-first: confirm the OAuth round-trip before any UI polish. Then rendering. Then the surface's actual shape — the face it turns toward the co-op's members.

The question we're sitting with: what does it mean for organizational memory to be owned by the people it belongs to? The vault is a cooperative artifact. The surface should be one too — not a product you subscribe to, but an instrument the co-op builds for itself, shaped around how it actually works.

Τέχνη. The knowledge embodied in making something well. In this case: a face for what the co-op knows.
